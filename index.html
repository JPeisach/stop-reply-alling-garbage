Stop reply-alling garbage

This website is still under construction. Feel free to contribute

Every now and then, an email will be sent out to a large group of people. This can happen in many contexts, but lets look at a school, for example.

Subject: Attendance Makeup
From: Principal (principal@school.org)
To: Students (students@school.org)
CC: Teachers (teachers@school.org)

This is a reminder that students that are absent for more than 14 days will have to make up credit after school, and may not get their diploma at graduation. Some days are available: June 13th - 17th, July 2nd - 5th, and August 3rd - 8th.

If you need to make up attendance, please email me for which dates work best.

Mr. Davis

Someone (unknowningly, or knowingly if they want to create trouble) will make the simple mistake of pressing "Reply-All" instead of "Reply":

Subject: Re: Attendance Makeup
From: Seymour Butts (sbutts@school.org)
To: Principal (principal@school.org)
CC: Students (students@school.org), Teachers (teachers@school.org)

Hi Mr. Davis, can I make up during the week of July 2nd?

Thanks!

Seymour

Seymour made a mistake. Now everybody knows that he is chronically missing school. All 200+ students of the school, and all the teachers: even the ones that Seymour doesn't have. This could hurt his reputation.

Now, when he realizes this mistake, he will never make it again. What you should do at this point, if you are any observer or accidental email recipient, is to ignore the email. If you're nice, consider telling them privately about the mistake they made, especially if they are a repeat offender.

But, often, mail groups can have a sense of "casualty" to them. There could be an accidental reply-all to a school club email, or a group of friends. Regardless of the context, what you should NOT do is reply-all in response, even if you are trying to be polite. This often occurs:

Subject: Re: Re: Attendance Makeup
From: Al Coholic (acoholic@school.org)
To: Seymour Butts (sbutts@school.org), Principal (principal@school.org)
CC: Students (students@school.org), Teachers (teachers@school.org)

By the way, you just emailed the entire grade.

Al

Or, even worse:

Subject: Re: Re: Attendance Makeup
From: Oliver Klosoff (oklosoff@school.org)
To: Seymour Butts (sbutts@school.org), Principal (principal@school.org)
CC: Students (students@school.org), Teachers (teachers@school.org)

Lol, nice one Seymour!

Al

And the worst of all:

Subject: Re: Re: Attendance Makeup
From: Anita Bath (abath@school.org)
To: Seymour Butts (sbutts@school.org), Principal (principal@school.org)
CC: Students (students@school.org), Teachers (teachers@school.org)

(A meme or image of some kind)

Anita

By doing this, you are doing multiple things. First, youa re making yourself look like a fool. You're making fun of someone reply-alling, by reply-alling. Even if you meant it in good faith, it is.. annoying.

In many cases, you are also risking a bit of a chain reaction, where someone receiving the email will think:

Eura Snotball:

Hm, this person used reply-all, and they didn't get in trouble.

Or:

Haywood U. Cuddleme:

Wow, my friend sent this really funny message/meme/joke to EVERYBODY. I think I should comment on this, or maybe share a meme in response to that because it's funny!

So then, one person replies-all. Then another person does it. And eventually, any meaningful form of communication stops. It seems funny. But this leads to a bunch of garbage being sent around at rapid speeds, often creating a large email chain.

This may seem harmless, but remember, by hitting Reply-All, you are replying to EVERYONE who received the original email. Including people who do not care.

When you send an email, aside from the usual things, like, assuming that you thought about what you typed before sending it, and that you are being respectful - all the usual deets - you are sending a message to someone's individual mailbox. While you may not use email seriously, the other person might. In the example shown above, most likely the teachers use email for serious purposes. Not as a s--tposting ground.

So, the people who ensure they have all their emails read, so they stay on top of things, will be interrupted by a notification from their email software saying that they have a new email that they MUST read in order to keep the number of unread emails at "None". So by the rules of technology, they MUST click and read your garbage that they do not care about, nor do they find funny.

Believe it or not, this is actually disruptive. Some people need to have notifications on because there are ACTUALLY IMPORTANT things that they need to be aware of, as soon as they are informed of it. A non-exhaustive list of contexts that this person can find themself in to read your stupid email, only to have a loud *ding* bother them:

  • Last-minute studying for an exam
  • Trying to give a presentation
  • Taking care of a task, being *really* focused on it
  • Having had a long day
  • Having a bunch of emails that the person needs to catch up on
  • Driving in the car
  • Standing in public
  • Being stressed

And so, they must stop what they are doing, open their email, click on the message, only to find your meaningless trash email. You just distracted that person, pissed them off, and possibly just took a hit on your respect, reputation, and maturity.

This also may make it difficult for people to actually respond to emails seriously; depending on the mail client, if someone sends a long message (like the entire "Bee Movie" script - yes, this has happened before), it creates a bunch of client lag. Additionally, the original email is buried under a heap of trash, so the person needs to scroll all the way up to find the message they want to respond to. Even worse, the message may be sandwiched between emails of garbage.

There is a simple solution here: Don't. Reply. All. Garbage. People rely on email for serious use. We don't need your memes, your jokes, your funnies (unless it is asked for). By doing this, you are making the world a better place.

FAQ

    • Q: But it is really funny!
      A: It can be at first, but when it carries on so log, it becomes more disruptive than helpful.

    • Q: Can't they just mute the email?
      A: Yes, they can. But if an actual important response is sent, then the person won't see. And again, it takes time to manually mute the email. Don't make people waste their time. Life is short.

    • Q: Why not just block me?
      A: Because, again, what if there is an actual important email?

    • Q: You're no fun.
      A: Life isn't fun.

Conclusion

So the next time you see someone reply-all, or find the opportunity to do so, think:

  • Am I sending meaningful information to an appropriate audience?
  • By sending this, will I be annoying others?
  • What will people think of me if I send this email?
  • Is this really worth sending to everybody, or am I being annoying?
